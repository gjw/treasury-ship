# Voice Guide — Gauntlet Deliverables

## Audience Model

The grading process is human-AI synthesis. The human grader feeds the report to an AI
for summarization, spot-checks the original, and asks follow-up questions. This means:

- Structure and semantic precision matter most — the AI intermediary parses claims cleanly.
- The human sees at least parts of it, so it should read as a professional document.
- Every metric must be in a table or on its own line — never buried in a paragraph.

## Rules

1. **No internal role names in deliverables.** "Chair," "Tower," "Trench," "Herald,"
   "Warden," "Scout" are internal vocabulary. Use neutral descriptions: "planning agent,"
   "implementation agent," "writing agent" — or no labels at all.

2. **No grader-speak.** Never mention scoring, rubrics, graders, or evaluators in
   deliverables. Frame all decisions in terms of real users, engineering judgment, and
   production impact. The thinking can be grader-aware; the writing cannot.

3. **Address repeated feedback head-on.** When a grader repeats a note across multiple
   checkpoints (e.g., "where's Lighthouse?"), treat it as a signal even if the note is
   technically wrong. Surface the answer prominently rather than hoping they'll read
   more carefully next time.

4. **Concise, not empty.** Let numbers speak. No fluff, no filler, no "in conclusion."
   But longer output is fine when the content is substantive — a thorough reflection
   section is better than a thin one.

5. **Explain scoring path choices.** When an assignment offers alternative targets
   (e.g., "10+ Lighthouse improvement OR fix all serious violations"), explicitly state
   which path was chosen and why the other wasn't viable. Don't leave the grader to
   figure out which target you were aiming at.

## LinkedIn Voice

- **Shorter beats longer.** ~150-160 words, not 220. Punchier is better.
- **"What made this interesting/hard" framing** — not "I did X, Y, Z" but why X was
  a different kind of hard. Show the thinking, not just the output.
- **No hashtags, no emoji.** Ever.
- **Specific numbers and tech names** — not vague. "312 KB of TipTap JSON" not
  "large API responses."
- **Reference the program naturally** — "Gauntlet for America" inline, not @tags.
- **End with momentum** — what's next, what shifted, where you're heading.
- **Honest about difficulty** — "reading code carefully is a different kind of hard
  than writing it from scratch" is the register. Not boastful, not humble-braggy.
